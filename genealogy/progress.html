My Genealogical Progress

My favorite hobby, for a while now

Genealogy has been a passion of mine since elementary school. I've spent almost fifteen years researching my family history, and learning local histories, research methods, how to read 19th-century Hungarian cursive, and countless other little skills and tricks to improve my family history research.

The all-important Record Ratio

A simple metric helps keep my research thorough

I've spent a lot of time researching Ancestry hints, sifting through microfiche, scanning census records, cemetery rolls, WWII draft cards, and so on. All of these "hints" help to frame a clearer picture of the members of the family trees I've built.

Many of these "hints" are simply vital records, newspaper clippings, family Bible records, or other important and reliable accounts that can provide information on the family's history and ancestry. As I've committed myself to upholding a strong standard of data integrity in my family trees, I set a general standard of three records per person, on average. This rough guildeline isn't a perfect reflection of the tree's accuracy, rather it gives a small overview of how thoroughly researched the average tree member is.

My first family tree turned into a giant mess. It was filled with weakly sourced profiles, unreliable data, people that never existed or dates that could never have aligned. None of this was intentional - it's because I didn't take enough time to make sure the data I committed to my tree was truly...true.

Since I've completely rebuilt the tree, I've used the record ratio as a way to make sure I'm not adding things willy-nilly and that each member of the family tree is given the appropriate consideration and research. That three-records-per-person threshold is what initially inspired me to restart my tree and start things fresh.
